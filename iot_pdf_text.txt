Abstract
The consumption of expired medicines contributes to over 125,000 preventable
deaths annually in India, driven primarily by the absence of automated monitor-
ing systems in household and clinical settings. This project presents theSmart
Medicine Cabinet T racker (SMCT)—an Internet of Things (IoT) based em-
bedded system built on the Arduino Uno R3 platform—that monitors medicine
expiry dates, provides RFID-based individual medicine identification, and deliv-
ers real-time environmental condition alerts through a multi-sensor intelligent plat-
form. The system integrates an MFRC522 RFID reader, DS1307 Real-Time Clock
(RTC), DHT22 temperature and humidity sensor, 16×2 I2C LCD display, 4×4
matrix keypad, and a three-colour LED indicator array. The system successfully
identifies registered medicines, computes days-remaining-until-expiry in real time,
detects unsafe storage conditions, and triggers a three-tier graduated alert scheme
(SAFE/WARNING/CRITICAL). All medicine data is stored persistently in EEP-
ROM, ensuring retention across power cycles. The complete hardware prototype
is achievable below Rs. 800, making deployment viable for household use across
all economic segments. Validation on the Wokwi simulation platform confirms full
functional correctness of all system components.
Keywords:IoT, RFID, Arduino, Medicine Management, Expiry Detection, Smart
Cabinet, DHT22, EEPROM, Embedded Systems, Healthcare Automation, DS1307
RTC, Alert Classification

I. Introduction
The management of medicines in both household and clinical environments presents a
persistent and largely unresolved challenge. Studies conducted across Indian households
reveal that approximately 70% of homes currently store at least one expired medicine,
while national data indicates that consumption of expired pharmaceutical products con-
tributes to over 125,000 preventable deaths annually [1]. The primary cause of this prob-
lem is the absence of an automated, intelligent monitoring system capable of proactively
alerting users before medicines reach or exceed their expiry dates.
Traditional medicine storage relies entirely on manual inspection—a process that is
inherently unreliable due to cognitive limitations, time constraints, and the physical de-
terioration of printed expiry date labels over time. Healthcare institutions face addi-
tional challenges including the management of large inventories, the rotation of stock on
a first-expiry-first-out basis, and compliance with pharmaceutical storage temperature
requirements.
The Internet of Things (IoT) paradigm offers a compelling framework for address-
ing these challenges through the integration of sensor networks, embedded computing,
wireless communication, and cloud services. RFID technology in particular provides a
non-contact, automatic identification mechanism well suited to medicine tracking appli-
cations, offering unique identification of individual items without requiring line-of-sight
or manual scanning [2].
This paper presents theSmart Medicine Cabinet Tracker (SMCT)—a complete
embedded system prototype that demonstrates core technical capabilities required for
intelligent medicine management. The system was designed with three primary objec-
tives: (1) reliable RFID-based identification of individual medicine boxes, (2) real-time
expiry date computation using a hardware RTC, and (3) environmental storage condition
monitoring with automated alerts.
II. Problem Statement
The following five critical problems are addressed by the proposed system:
P1 – Expired Medicine Consumption:Approximately 70% of Indian households
store expired medicines due to lack of monitoring systems, leading to health complications
and preventable deaths.
P2 – Manual Monitoring Failure:Printed expiry labels fade, are misread, or are
overlooked entirely in cabinets with multiple medicines. No low-cost automated solution
currently exists for household use.
P3 – Unsafe Storage Conditions:Many medicines require storage below 25°C
and below 60% relative humidity. Kitchens and bathrooms—common storage locations—
frequently exceed these thresholds.

The system is built around an Arduino Uno R3 microcontroller operating at 16MHz with
32KB of Flash memory, 2KB of SRAM, and 1KB of EEPROM. The Arduino serves as
the central processing unit coordinating all peripheral components through SPI, I2C, and
GPIO communication protocols simultaneously.
The hardware architecture follows ahub-and-spoke topologywith the Arduino at the
centre and six peripheral subsystems connected via dedicated communication channels
(Fig.1). TheRFIDsubsystemusestheSPIprotocoloperatingatthemaximumsupported
frequency of 10MHz. The display and clock subsystems share the I2C bus at 100kHz with
unique 7-bit addresses (0x27 for LCD, 0x68 for RTC). The environmental monitoring,
keypad input, and indicator subsystems use direct GPIO connections.

IV. Component Descriptions and Characteristics
A. Arduino Uno R3 – Central Microcontroller
The Arduino Uno R3 is an open-source microcontroller board based on the Microchip
ATmega328P. It provides 14 digital I/O pins (6 with PWM output), 6 analogue input
pins, a 16MHz crystal oscillator, and a USB connection for programming. The on-chip
1KB EEPROM provides non-volatile storage for medicine records without any external
memory chip. Operating voltage is 5V; Flash memory is 32KB (0.5KB used by boot-
loader); SRAM is 2KB. The SPI and I2C buses are used simultaneously in SMCT: SPI
for RFID at 10MHz, I2C for RTC and LCD at 100kHz.

B. MFRC522 RFID Reader/Writer Module
TheMFRC522isahighlyintegratedcontactlesscommunicationICoperatingat13.56MHz[3].
It supports the ISO/IEC 14443 A/MIFARE protocol, enabling reading of standard RFID
cards, tags, and key fobs. Operating voltage is 3.3V; reading distance is up to 5–6cm;
SPI clock supports up to 10MHz; the onboard FIFO buffer is 64 bytes. Each RFID
tag carries a unique 4-byte (32-bit) UID assembled into auint32_tvalue used as the
primary EEPROM lookup key. Pin connections: SDA→D10, SCK→D13, MOSI→D11,
MISO→D12, RST→D9.
C. DS1307 Real-Time Clock Module
The DS1307 is an I2C real-time clock/calendar providing seconds, minutes, hours, day,
date, month, and year information. A battery-backed CR2032 coin cell maintains time-
keeping even when the main supply is removed (typical 10-year lifespan). I2C address is
0x68; accuracy is±2ppm (0°C–40°C); 56 bytes of non-volatile RAM are available. The
firmware converts both stored expiry date and current RTC date to Unix timestamp for-
mat to compute the exact number of days remaining, correctly handling leap years and
month-length variations.

humidity via a single-wire serial interface. Temperature range:−40to+80°C (±0.5°C
accuracy); humidity range: 0–100% RH (±2–5% RH); sampling period: 0.5Hz. Based on

ing. Key assignments: 0–9 for digits; A–D for letters; # to confirm; * for backspace; A
key to enter REGISTER mode; B key to cancel; C key for manual status check; D key
for caregiver summary display.

B. Medicine Registration Process
Registration begins when the user presses keyA, transitioning from MONITOR to REG-
ISTER mode. The system then follows a 3-step workflow: (1) RFID tag scan—the 4-byte
UID is read and assembled into a 32-bit integer; (2) name entry—alphanumeric input via
keypad with * for backspace and # to confirm; (3) expiry date (DDMMYYYY) followed
by optional dose time (HHMM). On final confirmation, theMedstructure is written
to EEPROM usingEEPROM.put()and the system returns automatically to MONITOR
mode. PressingBat any step cancels without saving.

D. Environmental Monitoring Subsystem
The DHT22 is polled every 30 seconds. If temperature exceeds 30°C or humidity exceeds
70% RH, a storage alert fires: the LCD shows!!STORAGE ALERT!, the yellow LED ac-
tivates, the buzzer sounds, andstorageWarn = trueis set for all registered medicines.
The duration of bad conditions is accumulated inbadHoursand written to EEPROM for
persistent logging across power cycles.
E. Dose Tracking and Caregiver Notification
Each medicine can have a daily dose schedule (stored asdoseHour/doseMin). The dose-
check routine runs every 60 seconds. If the current RTC hour matchesdoseHourand
the RFID tag was scanned (medicine removed),doseTakenis set true and aDOSE_TAKEN
JSON event is emitted on the Serial port at 115200baud. If the hour passes without a
scan,missedDosesincrements and aMISSED_DOSEJSON event is emitted. All flags reset
at midnight. A JSON heartbeat is transmitted every 10 seconds:

This stream is designed for connection to an ESP8266 WiFi or HC-05 Bluetooth
module, forwarding events to a caregiver mobile application.

VII. Future Integration and Large-Scale Applications
A. WiFi and Mobile Application Integration
The immediate next step is the addition of an ESP8266 or ESP32 WiFi module connected
to the Arduino Serial TX pin. The module forwards JSON events via MQTT protocol
to Firebase or ThingSpeak cloud, enabling push notifications for approaching expiry and
missed doses on a caregiver smartphone application.
B. Hospital and Clinical Deployment
At clinical scale, the architecture extends to manage hundreds of medicine bins using a
network of Arduino nodes connected to a central Raspberry Pi server running a Flask-
based inventory management dashboard, with automated FEFO (First-Expiry-First-Out)
rotation alerts and regulatory compliance reports.
C. Machine Learning and Predictive Analytics
A machine learning layer operating on accumulated usage data would enable predictive
restocking—training on historical removal timestamps to forecast medicine depletion days
in advance and automatically generate pharmacy orders.
D. Cold Chain Pharmaceutical Monitoring
Replacing the DHT22 with a precision thermocouple and integrating a GPS module
enablescontinuoustemperatureloggingthroughoutthepharmaceuticalsupplychainfrom
manufacturer to patient, with blockchain-backed immutable audit trails.
